Choosing colors for a design project is both the most exciting and the most intimidating part of the creative process. A color palette generator takes the guesswork out of color selection by applying proven color theory principles automatically.

Types of Color Schemes

Monochromatic

A single hue used at different saturations and lightness levels. This is the easiest scheme to get right — it's always harmonious because every color shares the same base. Use a monochromatic scheme for clean, professional designs where you want to convey simplicity and sophistication.

Best for: Corporate websites, data visualizations, minimalist branding.

Complementary

Two colors opposite each other on the color wheel (like blue and orange, or red and green). This creates maximum contrast and visual tension. Use complementary colors sparingly — one as the dominant color and the other as an accent.

Best for: Calls to action, hero sections, anything that needs to pop.

Analogous

Three colors that sit next to each other on the color wheel. These schemes feel harmonious and natural, like a sunset or a forest scene. They lack the contrast of complementary schemes but excel at creating mood.

Best for: Backgrounds, branding, nature-themed designs.

Triadic

Three colors evenly spaced on the color wheel (like red, yellow, and blue). Triadic schemes are vibrant and balanced. Use one color as the dominant and the other two as accents to avoid visual chaos.

Best for: Children's products, playful brands, creative portfolios.

Building a Full Palette

A great design palette typically includes 4–6 colors:

  • 2–3 primary colors: The main identity colors
  • 1–2 secondary/accent colors: For contrast and highlights
  • 2–3 neutral colors: Whites, grays, blacks for backgrounds and text

Most successful designs follow the 60-30-10 rule: 60% dominant color, 30% secondary, 10% accent.

Testing Your Palette

Before committing to a palette, test it in context:

  • Accessibility: Check contrast ratios for text readability
  • Color blindness: Simulate how your palette looks to color-blind users
  • Grayscale: Remove color to check if the design still works
  • Real content: Apply the palette to actual content, not just swatches

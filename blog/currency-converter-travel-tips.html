Currency Converter Tips for Travel: Save Money on Exchange Rates

Published Jun 14, 2026 · 7 min read

If you've ever traveled internationally, you've probably been burned by bad exchange rates. The rate you see on Google and the rate your bank gives you are often very different — and that difference costs you real money. A currency converter helps you understand the true cost before you transact.

Understanding Exchange Rates

The Mid-Market Rate

This is the "real" exchange rate that banks and financial institutions use when trading with each other. It's the rate you see on financial websites. You will almost never get this rate as a consumer — there's always a markup.

The Buy/Sell Spread

Currency exchanges quote two rates: the "buy" rate (what they'll pay for your currency) and the "sell" rate (what they'll charge you). The difference is their profit. A fair spread is 1–3% off the mid-market rate. Airport kiosks often charge 8–15%.

Where to Exchange Currency

Best Options

  • ATMs abroad: Usually the best rate if your bank doesn't charge foreign transaction fees. Withdraw in the local currency, not your home currency (avoid Dynamic Currency Conversion).
  • Credit cards with no foreign fees: Cards from Capital One, Chase Sapphire, and others offer near-market rates on purchases. Always choose to pay in the local currency.
  • Local banks at home: Order currency before you travel. Rates are better than airport exchange booths.

Worst Options

  • Airport exchange counters: Convenient, but rates are terrible. Avoid unless it's an emergency.
  • Hotel front desks: Similar markup to airports.
  • Street exchange vendors: Risk of counterfeit and scams alongside bad rates.

Avoiding Dynamic Currency Conversion (DCC)

When you pay by card abroad, the terminal may ask if you want to pay in your home currency instead of the local one. Always say no. DCC uses a terrible exchange rate (often 4–7% markup) compared to the 0–1% your card network would charge.

Budgeting with Currency Fluctuations

Exchange rates change daily. A trip that costs 1,000 EUR might be $1,080 today but $1,120 next week. Use our currency converter to check the rate before and during your trip, and add a 10% buffer to your budget for rate fluctuations.

Digital Alternatives

Consider travel-friendly digital banks like Revolut, Wise (formerly TransferWise), or Monzo. These offer near-market exchange rates, instant currency conversion, and often no foreign transaction fees. They're a game-changer for international travel.
